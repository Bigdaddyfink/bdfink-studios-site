01 — Brand Overview

Who We Are

We transform ideas into motion and ink into impact — delivering creative solutions that move audiences, whether on screen or on paper.

Big Daddy Fink Studios — known as BDFink Studios — is a creative services studio founded by Timmy Harris, a SCAD-trained artist and designer with over 26 years of professional experience. The name carries the swagger of rock poster art and the grit of underground illustration, while the work behind it spans everything from Fortune 500 marketing to limited-edition screen prints.

From 16 years leading multimedia design at IBM to producing marketing content for brands at Techtronic Industries, the studio brings enterprise-level polish to every project — while never losing the creative fire that fuels its gig poster art and illustration work.

Today, BDFink Studios operates at the intersection of traditional craft and emerging technology, incorporating generative AI tools into its creative pipeline while maintaining the hand-crafted sensibility that makes each piece distinctive.

Whether it's a social media campaign that needs to stop the scroll, a motion graphics package that elevates a brand, or a limited-edition gig poster that becomes a collector's piece — the work is always driven by the same principle: make it matter.

Logo Elements

The illustrated logo is a distinctive brand asset with several key components that should be preserved in all usage.

Typography Lockup

"Big Daddy" in bold, blocky lowercase letters. "Fink" in outlined/dimensional lettering. "Studios" in flowing script. The mixed typographic styles are intentional — they reflect the studio's range.

Self-Portrait Character

The illustrated self-portrait in the golden circular frame is a core brand element. It personalizes the studio and establishes the hand-drawn aesthetic immediately.

Mascot

The brain creature with the red top hat is the studio mascot. It adds personality and works as a standalone mark for social media avatars, stickers, and watermarks.

The logo uses deep maroon/red, black, white, and gold — tones that naturally align with the brand's Ember and Midnight palette. These colors anchor the brand across all touchpoints.

Logo Rules

Clear Space

Maintain a minimum clear space around the logo equal to the height of the letter "B" on all sides. No other elements should encroach on this space.

Minimum Size

The logo should never be displayed smaller than 120px wide on screen or 1.5 inches in print to maintain legibility.

Color Integrity

Use only approved color combinations. On dark backgrounds, use white text with Ember accent. On light backgrounds, use Midnight text with Ember accent.

Don't Distort

Never stretch, compress, rotate, add effects (drop shadows, outlines, gradients), or alter the proportions of the logo or its animated version.

Usage Guidelines

Instrument Serif — Display & Headings

  • Use for all display text, page titles, and section headings
  • Weight: 400 (regular) only — the serif's elegance comes from its natural form
  • Line-height: 1.05–1.15 for headings
  • Use italic sparingly for emphasis or creative accent
  • Never use below 1.1rem — switch to DM Sans for smaller sizes

DM Sans — Body & Interface

  • Use for all body copy, navigation, buttons, labels, and UI text
  • Weights: 300 (light), 400 (regular), 500 (medium), 600 (semibold), 700 (bold)
  • Line-height: 1.5–1.7 for body text
  • Letter-spacing: 0.01em for body, 0.1–0.15em for uppercase labels
  • Use uppercase + letter-spacing for section labels and metadata

Do & Don't

✓ Do

  • "I've spent 26 years solving creative problems — here's how I'd approach yours."
  • "Your video needs to stop the scroll in 3 seconds. I know how to make that happen."
  • "I handle the full pipeline: editing, motion graphics, color, sound, and delivery."
  • "Let's talk about what you need and find the right approach."

✗ Don't

  • "I bring a wealth of expertise and a proven track record of delivering high-quality work."
  • "We provide compelling solutions leveraging our synergistic capabilities."
  • "Creative Thinker and Dreamer" (as a professional title)
  • "I've been getting into new stuff like Generative AI applications."

Context Switching

Corporate & Proposals

Professional, results-focused, specific. Emphasize deliverables, timelines, and measurable outcomes. Reference Fortune 500 experience and enterprise-level process.

"With 16 years of multimedia production at IBM and post-production leadership at TTI, I bring enterprise-grade quality and reliability to every project."

Creative Community

Authentic, direct, fellow-artist energy. Emphasize craft, collaboration, and creative process. Let the art and illustration portfolio do the heavy lifting.

"I've been drawing gig posters and concert art for over a decade. Each one is hand-crafted, limited edition, and designed to end up on someone's wall — not in the trash."

Social Media

Casual but intentional. Show process, behind-the-scenes, and finished work. Short, punchy captions. Let visuals lead.

"New poster drop for @bandname. Three-color screen print, edition of 50. Link in bio."

Resume & Cover Letters

Confident and specific. Lead with results and capabilities, not generic descriptors. Every sentence should either prove competence or demonstrate fit.

"Led end-to-end post-production for TTI's product launch campaigns, managing timelines across internal teams and external vendors to deliver on-brand video assets."
06 — Visual Style

How We Look

Visual guidelines that maintain consistency across both the video/motion portfolio and the illustration/design portfolio.

Video & Motion Thumbnails

  • Dark, cinematic framing — pull the most dynamic frame
  • Consistent aspect ratio: 16:9 for video thumbnails
  • Minimal text overlay — let the image speak
  • If text is needed, use DM Sans Bold in White or Ember
  • Subtle vignette or grade to unify thumbnail sets
  • Pair thumbnails in 2-up grids on portfolio pages

Illustration & Poster Art

  • Present at full resolution with clean borders
  • White or Midnight background behind artwork — never busy
  • For portfolio: show the art straight, no mock-ups unless requested
  • When showing print work, photograph on neutral surfaces
  • Color accuracy matters — calibrate displays when possible
  • Hand-drawn line quality is a brand signature — preserve it

Photography Treatment

  • Desaturated, slightly cool grade for behind-the-scenes photos
  • High contrast, editorial feel — not Instagram-filtered
  • Grain is acceptable and reinforces the analog-meets-digital brand
  • Avoid overprocessed HDR or heavy vignettes
  • Crop intentionally — asymmetric compositions over centered shots

Graphic Elements

  • Use thin rules (1px) in Smoke or Border color for dividers
  • Ember accent lines or bars for emphasis (2–3px weight)
  • Rounded corners: 10–16px for cards, 6px for small elements
  • Subtle gradients (radial, low opacity) for atmospheric depth
  • No drop shadows — use border and background contrast instead
